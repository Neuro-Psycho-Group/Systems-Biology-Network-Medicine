Section 1 · Layer 0

The biology of schizophrenia

Schizophrenia affects ~1% of the global population, with heritability of 60–80%. It is fundamentally a disorder of synaptic connectivity driven by the interplay of genetic vulnerability and environmental insults across neurodevelopment. Here are the four major neurobiological hypotheses — the foundation of your Layer 0 literature review.

⚡
HYPOTHESIS 01

Dopamine Hypothesis v3.0

Diverse genetic and environmental risks converge on elevated presynaptic dopamine synthesis in the associative striatum (Howes & Kapur, 2009). PET imaging confirms increased [¹⁸F]-DOPA uptake even pre-symptom onset. Striatal hyperdopaminergia → aberrant salience → positive symptoms. Prefrontal cortical hypodopaminergia → negative/cognitive symptoms.

🔗
HYPOTHESIS 02

Glutamate / NMDA Hypofunction

NMDA receptor antagonists (PCP, ketamine) reproduce the full SCZ symptom spectrum — including negative and cognitive symptoms. The model: NMDA hypofunction on GABAergic interneurons → disinhibition of pyramidal neurons. SCHEMA (2022) provided genomic proof: GRIN2A and GRIA3 carry exome-wide significant mutations.

🧬
HYPOTHESIS 03

Neurodevelopmental Origin

Early insults create a latent vulnerability unmasked in adolescence when the PFC matures (Weinberger, 1987; Murray & Lewis, 1987). Supporting evidence: premorbid childhood cognitive deficits, structural brain changes at first episode, absence of classical neurodegeneration markers. The modern "multiple-hit" model recognises gene×environment accumulation.

🔥
HYPOTHESIS 04

Neuroinflammation & Complement

Sekar et al. (2016) showed SCZ's strongest MHC association arises from C4A structural variation. C4A mediates developmental synapse elimination; overexpression reduces cortical synapse density. ~⅓ of patients show a high-neuroinflammation profile. Complement cascade (C4A→C3→microglial CR3) links immune dysfunction directly to synaptic pruning.

Genetic architecture: from common to rare variants

📊
PGC3 GWAS (Trubetskoy et al., 2022, Nature)
76,755 cases · 243,649 controls · 287 genomic loci · 120 prioritised protein-coding genes · SNP-based heritability h²≈0.24. Enriched in excitatory/inhibitory CNS neurons and synaptic genes.
🧪
SCHEMA Exome (Singh et al., 2022, Nature)
24,248 cases · 97,322 controls · 10 genes with exome-wide significant ultra-rare variants (ORs 3–50): SETD1A, CUL1, GRIN2A, GRIA3, TRIO, SP4, XPO7, CACNA1G, HERC1, RB1CC1
🔬
Key CNVs
22q11.2 deletion (>20-fold SCZ risk; ~22% carriers develop SCZ) · NRXN1 (2p16.3) · 15q13.3 · 16p11.2 · 1q21.1. Large effect, rare, mechanistically informative.

Section 2 · Protein Systems

Molecular pathways & protein systems

These are the protein systems you will work with in your PPI network. Click each pathway to explore key proteins — hover over any protein tag for its full name and significance.

Glutamate receptors form the core of SCZ's synaptic biology and the most direct link to genetic evidence. NMDA receptor subunits bind to PSD-95 via C-terminal PDZ domains, positioning them at the physical centre of postsynaptic protein complexes.

GRIN1NMDA receptor subunit 1 — obligatory subunit of all NMDA receptors. Binds glycine. Post-mortem studies show reduced NR1 protein in PFC. GRIN2ANMDA receptor subunit 2A — SCHEMA exome-wide significant gene (2022). Binds glutamate. High confidence STRING interactions ~200+ GRIN2BNMDA receptor subunit 2B — Major PGC/GWAS signal. Critical for synaptic plasticity and LTP. Important in DLPFC working memory circuits. GRIA1AMPA receptor subunit GluA1. Shows altered trafficking in SCZ. Regulated by phosphorylation at PSD. GRIA3AMPA receptor subunit GluA3 — SCHEMA exome-wide significant. X-linked. Essential for AMPA receptor assembly. GRM3Metabotropic glutamate receptor 3 — GWAS hit. Presynaptic autoreceptor regulating glutamate release. Active clinical trial target.

Key finding: de novo mutations in GRIN2A appear in both common-variant GWAS and rare-variant exome studies — an "allelic series" directly implicating glutamatergic dysfunction.

💡
STRING tip: GRIN1, GRIN2A, GRIN2B form a tight interaction cluster in STRING. Use these as your core seed nodes for glutamatergic PPI analysis. All have 100+ high-confidence interactions. Confidence threshold ≥0.7 recommended.

The postsynaptic density (PSD) is a ~1µm protein matrix beneath excitatory synapses. It contains ~1,000–1,500 proteins (Bayés et al., 2011) and is the primary convergence point for SCZ genetic risk — multiple GWAS, CNV, and exome studies converge here.

DLG4PSD-95 — the master scaffold of excitatory synapses. Three PDZ domains bind NMDA receptors, AMPA auxiliaries, and signalling enzymes. >200 high-confidence STRING interactions. Best seed node for a SCZ network. SHANK1Synaptic scaffold protein 1. Cross-links Homer and PSD-95 complexes. De novo CNVs linked to SCZ and autism. SHANK2Scaffold protein linking glutamate receptors to cytoskeletal elements. Differentially expressed in SCZ proteomics. SHANK3Synaptic scaffold — differentially expressed in SCZ PSD proteomics (Focking et al. 2015). De novo CNVs implicated in both autism and SCZ. HOMER1Homer scaffold protein 1. Modulates metabotropic glutamate receptor (mGluR) signalling. Variants associated with SCZ symptom severity. DLGAP1GKAP/SAPAP1 — links DLG4 to SHANK proteins. Central linker in the DLG4→GKAP→SHANK→Homer chain.

The signalling cascade PSD-95 (DLG4) → GKAP/DLGAP → SHANK → Homer is a well-characterised protein complex directly tractable for STRING analysis. De novo mutations in SCZ are significantly overrepresented in the ARC complex and NMDAR complex of the PSD (Fromer et al., 2014).

GABAergic interneuron dysfunction is among the most replicated post-mortem findings in SCZ. Parvalbumin-positive (PV+) fast-spiking basket cells in DLPFC layer III are most affected. These interneurons generate gamma oscillations critical for working memory.

PVALBParvalbumin — calcium-binding protein expressed in PV+ fast-spiking interneurons. Consistently reduced immunoreactivity in SCZ DLPFC (neuron loss is debated; marker reduction is replicated in 25+ studies). GAD1Glutamate decarboxylase 1 (GAD67) — the enzyme that synthesises GABA. The most replicated post-mortem finding in SCZ: reduced expression in DLPFC in 25+ independent studies. GAD2Glutamate decarboxylase 2 (GAD65) — the other GABA synthesis enzyme. Localised to axon terminals; reduced in SCZ alongside GAD67. SSTSomatostatin — marker for a second subtype of inhibitory interneuron. Pronounced deficits in cortical layers 2–3 and 5–6 in SCZ DLPFC. SLC6A1GAT-1, GABA transporter — removes GABA from synaptic cleft. Reduced expression in SCZ DLPFC reduces GABA reuptake efficiency. GABRB2GABA-A receptor β2 subunit — GWAS-associated. Pocklington et al. (2015) showed SCZ CNVs enrich GABA-A receptor complexes alongside glutamatergic complexes.

Proposed mechanism: NMDA receptor hypofunction on pyramidal cells reduces excitatory drive to PV+ interneurons → GAD67 downregulation → impaired gamma oscillations → cognitive dysfunction.

Dopamine signalling remains the primary pharmacological target in SCZ. All effective antipsychotics block D2 receptors. However, dopamine dysregulation is likely downstream of glutamatergic dysfunction rather than the root cause.

DRD2Dopamine receptor D2 — GWAS hit (PGC, 2014). Target of ALL antipsychotic medications. Striatal D2 blockade treats positive symptoms but not negative/cognitive ones. DRD1Dopamine receptor D1 — expressed highly in DLPFC. Hypofunction contributes to working memory deficits. Target for new procognitive agents. AKT1Protein kinase B — downstream of DRD2, modulated by dopamine. Impaired AKT1 signalling in SCZ demonstrated by Emamian et al. (2004, Nature Genetics). GWAS-associated. GSK3BGlycogen synthase kinase 3β — phosphorylated by AKT1. Hyperactive in SCZ; connects dopamine, Wnt, and neurotrophin pathways. Target of lithium. COMTCatechol-O-methyltransferase — degrades dopamine in PFC (unlike striatum which uses DAT). Val158Met polymorphism affects prefrontal dopamine levels and working memory.

Complement-mediated synaptic pruning provides a direct molecular mechanism linking immune dysfunction to synapse loss. Sekar et al. (2016) showed C4A structural variation drives the MHC association with SCZ.

C4AComplement component 4A — key gene from Sekar et al. (2016, Nature). Alleles generating greater C4A expression proportionally increase SCZ risk. Tags synapses for microglial elimination. C3Complement component 3 — downstream of C4A. Deposits C3b onto synapses marking them for phagocytosis by microglia (via CR3 receptor). ITGAMIntegrin αM (CD11b) — part of microglial CR3 receptor complex. Mediates engulfment of C3b-tagged synapses. Target of potential synaptic pruning interventions. IL6Interleukin-6 — consistently elevated in SCZ patient serum and CSF. Levels correlate with reduced grey matter volume and poorer treatment response. TNFTumor necrosis factor α — pro-inflammatory cytokine elevated in SCZ. Modulates synaptic AMPA receptor trafficking and contributes to cortical circuit disruption. IL1BInterleukin-1β — elevated in CSF of SCZ patients. Part of the NLRP3 inflammasome pathway activated in neuroinflammatory SCZ subtype.

The "Type 2" SCZ subtype (Bowen et al., 2019) — ~55% of patients — shows marked upregulation of complement cascade genes in DLPFC, making this a high-priority module for PPI analysis.

Neurodevelopmental signalling pathways implicated in SCZ connect synaptic formation, neuronal migration, and brain wiring — all disrupted in early SCZ neurodevelopment.

NRXN1Neurexin-1 — trans-synaptic adhesion molecule. Most prevalent single-gene mutation in SCZ (recurrent deletions). Binds neuroligins, LRRTMs, cerebellins. >50 high-confidence STRING interactions. NLGN1Neuroligin-1 — binds neurexins to mediate synapse formation and maturation. Post-synaptic partner of NRXN1. DISC1Disrupted In Schizophrenia 1 — scaffold/hub protein with ~120 interactors. Identified via Scottish translocation pedigree. Does not reach GWAS significance alone but its interactome is enriched in GWAS analyses. BDNFBrain-derived neurotrophic factor — reduced in SCZ. Up to 80% of PV+ interneurons express TrkB; deficient BDNF→TrkB signalling is upstream of GABAergic marker reduction. NTRK2TrkB — BDNF receptor. Excellent Tier 1 STRING hub for SCZ networks. MTORmTOR — integrates neurotrophic, Wnt, and metabolic signalling. Connects AKT-GSK3β to synaptic protein synthesis. Well-curated STRING interactions. CTNNB1β-catenin — central mediator of Wnt signalling. Regulated by GSK3β phosphorylation. Connects Wnt pathway to synaptic function and gene expression.

Mitochondrial dysfunction is a major SCZ pathway — ~50% of altered proteins in SCZ brain tissue relate to mitochondrial function (Prabakaran et al., 2004). PV+ fast-spiking interneurons are especially vulnerable because of their high metabolic demands.

NDUFS1NADH-ubiquinone oxidoreductase core subunit — Complex I component. Reduced 18–35% in SCZ PFC. NDUFV2NADH dehydrogenase flavoprotein 2 — Complex I component. Reduced in SCZ; GWAS-associated in some studies. SOD2Superoxide dismutase 2 (mitochondrial) — key antioxidant enzyme. Reduced activity increases oxidative stress in PV+ interneurons in SCZ. GCLCGlutamate-cysteine ligase catalytic subunit — rate-limiting enzyme in glutathione synthesis. Reduced glutathione is consistently found in SCZ brain tissue. GPX1Glutathione peroxidase 1 — protects against oxidative damage. Reduced activity linked to PV+ interneuron vulnerability in SCZ.
⚠️
Key finding (Koopmans et al., 2026): In the most comprehensive PFC proteomics study to date (192 sections, 47 SCZ cases), mitochondrial oxidative respiration, ribosomes, and proteasome components were the most consistently downregulated categories. Kinases, small GTPases, and autophagy were upregulated.

Section 3 · Literature

Essential reading: PPI & network papers

These are the landmark papers applying network medicine to schizophrenia. ⭐ = must-read before starting your project. Sorted by tier of importance.

⭐ Foundational Network Medicine Frameworks

MUST READ
Nature Reviews Genetics
2011
Network medicine: a network-based approach to human disease
Barabási, Gulbahce & Loscalzo
Established the entire theoretical framework — disease modules, hub proteins, the human diseasome, and the "local hypothesis" that proteins interacting with disease proteins are themselves likely disease-related. 6,000+ citations.
🟢 Start here. Every concept in your project (hubs, modules, disease networks) is defined in this paper.
MUST READ
Science
2015
Uncovering disease-disease relationships through the incomplete interactome
Menche, Sharma, Kitsak, Ghiassian et al.
Mapped 299 diseases onto the protein interactome showing disease proteins form identifiable modules even though the interactome is ~80% incomplete. Introduces the network separation metric (sAB) for comparing disease modules.
🟢 Critical for any cross-disorder comparison (SCZ vs. bipolar, autism). Directly applicable to RQ4.

⭐ SCZ-Specific PPI Network Studies

MUST READ
npj Schizophrenia
2016
Schizophrenia interactome with 504 novel protein-protein interactions
Ganapathiraju et al.
Applied computational PPI prediction to 77 SCZ GWAS genes from PGC, discovering 365 novel PPIs. Built the "Schizo-Pi" network. 19 new interactors are drug targets (targeted by 130 existing drugs).
🟢 Most directly methodologically relevant to your project. Interactive tool: severus.dbmi.pitt.edu/schizo-pi
MUST READ
Nature
2014
De novo mutations in schizophrenia implicate synaptic networks
Fromer, Pocklington, Kavanagh et al.
Mapped de novo SNVs onto PSD protein complexes (ARC complex, NMDAR complex). Mutations overrepresented in PSD networks, overlapping with autism and intellectual disability. 1,500+ citations.
🟢 Created the paradigm of using PSD PPI networks as an analytical framework for SCZ genetics.
MUST READ
Nature Neuroscience
2011
Characterization of the proteome, diseases and evolution of the human postsynaptic density
Bayés, van de Lagemaat, Collins et al. (Seth Grant lab)
Proteomically isolated the human PSD, identifying ~1,461 proteins. Mutations in hPSD proteins cause 133 neurological/psychiatric diseases. This is the definitive PSD gene list.
🟢 The ~1,461 protein list is your critical seed set for any PSD-focused SCZ PPI project.
MUST READ
Molecular Psychiatry
2012
De novo CNV analysis implicates specific abnormalities of postsynaptic signalling complexes in SCZ
Kirov, Pocklington, Holmans et al.
First systematic demonstration that SCZ CNVs converge on PSD protein complexes: PSD proteome (P=1.72×10⁻⁶), NMDAR complex (P=4.24×10⁻⁶), ARC complex (P=3.78×10⁻⁸).
🟢 Established PSD-network analysis as a paradigm. Essential methods reference.
MUST READ
Neuron
2015
Novel Findings from CNVs Implicate Inhibitory and Excitatory Signaling Complexes in Schizophrenia
Pocklington, Rees, Walters et al.
Extended PSD analysis to GABAergic neurotransmission — first evidence that SCZ CNVs are enriched for GABA-A receptor complexes alongside glutamatergic signalling. PSD-95 enriched in deletions (P<10⁻⁷).
🟢 Bridges excitatory and inhibitory pathology; critical for RQ3 (bridge protein analysis).
MUST READ
Cell
2013
Spatial and Temporal Mapping of De Novo Mutations in SCZ to a Fetal Prefrontal Cortical Network
Gulsuner, Walsh, Watts et al.
Mapped de novo mutations onto BrainSpan developmental transcriptome data. In the DLPFC during fetal development, mutated genes formed both a co-expression network and a PPI network enriched for neuronal migration and synaptic transmission.
🟢 Uniquely combines spatial (DLPFC), temporal (fetal), and network dimensions.
MUST READ
Nature Neuroscience
2015
Psychiatric genome-wide association study analyses implicate neuronal, immune and histone pathways
Network and Pathway Analysis Subgroup of PGC
Applied 5 GWAS methods to SCZ, bipolar disorder, and depression (N=61,220). 49 significant pathways: (1) histone H3-K4 methylation, (2) neuronal/synaptic biology, (3) immune/neurotrophic pathways.
🟢 The official PGC pathway paper — defines the biological signal in GWAS data.
PLoS Computational Biology
2012

Jia, Wang, Fanous et al.
Used GWAS-weighted PPI network with Dense Module Search (DMS) on PINA database. Identified 205 module genes including DISC1, GNAI1, GRIN2B, enriched for neuronal processes.

Frontiers in Genetics
2018

Chen et al.
Common GWAS variants and rare de novo mutations converge on the same PPI networks, enriched for synaptic plasticity and NMDA receptor interactome genes. KEGG: LTP, neurotrophin signalling, ERBB.
🔵 Directly validates the rationale for combining common and rare variant seed genes.
iScience
2023

Pergola et al.
IP-MS for 6 SCZ risk genes in human induced cortical neurons. Network enriched for SCZ common variant risk and downregulated in layer 5/6 cortical neurons of SCZ patients. HCN1 sub-network enriched for rare PTVs.
🔵 State of the art: cell-type-specific PPI approaches beyond STRING.

Section 4 · Brain Region Focus

The DLPFC in schizophrenia

The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (BA9/46) is the most extensively studied region in SCZ. It is the seat of working memory and executive function — the very cognitive processes most impaired. Here's the molecular portrait of DLPFC pathology and the key datasets available.

Key molecular findings in DLPFC

↓23%
Dendritic spine density Reduced on deep layer III pyramidal neurons (Glantz & Lewis, 2000). Directly linked to reduced PSD protein expression.
↓25+
GAD67/PVALB studies Reduced GAD67 expression replicated in 25+ independent post-mortem studies of DLPFC layer III.
↓18–35%
Mitochondrial Complex I activity Reduced in PFC; Complex IV also 22–28% reduced. ATP synthesis reduced 20–22% (Koopmans et al., 2026).
3,092
DEGs in Type 2 SCZ subtype Bowen et al. (2019): ~55% of patients have 3,092 DLPFC DEGs. Type 1 (~45%) shows only 4 DEGs — near-normal transcriptome.
387
Differentially expressed proteins PFC proteomics (Koopmans et al., 2026): 192 sections, 47 SCZ vs 49 controls at 5% FDR.
🔑
Layer III vulnerability (Arnsten, 2022, Biological Psychiatry): DLPFC layer III synapses are uniquely vulnerable because they rely on NMDA (not AMPA) receptor neurotransmission and are subject to stress-induced calcium dysregulation. This molecular specificity explains why DLPFC is disproportionately affected.

→ CommonMind.org (Synapse)

→ Resource.PsychENCODE.org
Stanley Medical Research Institute

Deep proteomics of synaptic fractions. Aryal et al. (2023, Cell Reports): SCZ and bipolar show highly similar synaptic proteomic alterations. Shared downregulation of synaptic/mitochondrial function.
SynGO
1,112 curated synaptic genes · pre/postsynaptic annotations
Gold-standard synaptic gene ontology resource. Essential for enrichment analysis to determine whether your PPI network modules are pre- or postsynaptic.
→ syngoportal.org

Spatially resolved gene expression across DLPFC layers. Enables layer-specific validation of PPI network findings (critical given layer III vulnerability).

Section 5 · Practical Guide

Building your STRING-based PPI network

A step-by-step practical guide for the Layer 0 → Layer 1 → Layer 2 workflow. From curating your seed proteins to identifying hubs, running clustering algorithms, and interpreting enrichment results.

LAYER 0

Literature Review & Seed Curation

Compile a high-quality list of SCZ-associated seed genes from multiple evidence sources. Multi-source seeds produce more biologically meaningful networks than any single source alone.

# Recommended seed sources (total: ~100-500 genes) GWAS seeds: PGC3 120 prioritised genes → https://pgc.unc.edu/for-researchers/download-results/ DLPFC DEGs: CMC 693 DEGs (Fromer et al. 2016) BrainSeq 245 DEGs (Collado-Torres et al. 2019) PSD proteins: Bayés et al. 2011 ~1,461 proteins Synaptic: SynGO 1,112 curated genes Rare variants: SCHEMA 10 genes

Intersect sources to create a multi-evidence seed list. Document the provenance of each gene — this becomes crucial for interpreting your results.

LAYER 1

Seed-Only Network Construction

Input seed genes into STRING via Cytoscape stringApp. This produces a "zero-order" network showing only connections between your seed genes — the core disease interactome.

# STRING Settings for Layer 1: Confidence threshold: 0.7 (high confidence) or 0.9 (stringent) Additional interactors: 0 (seed-only mode) Network type: physical subnetwork for PPIs # After import in Cytoscape: Remove disconnected nodes Focus on largest connected component (LCC) Run Tools → Analyze Network (NetworkAnalyzer) Check degree distribution (should approximate scale-free)

Hub Identification — use multiple methods:

DEGREE
Number of direct interaction partners. Top 10% = hubs.
BETWEENNESS
Proportion of shortest paths through a node. High = "bottleneck" bridging modules.
MCC
Maximal Clique Centrality — best performance for essential protein prediction (CytoHubba plugin).
CLOSENESS
Inverse of average shortest-path distance. How quickly a protein can influence the whole network.
💡
Best practice: Take the intersection of top-ranked genes across multiple hub methods (MCC + Degree + Betweenness) for the most robust hub set. Published SCZ hubs: NRXN1, CACNA1C, GRIN2A, DRD2, DLG4, APP, UBC, YWHAZ.
LAYER 2

Extended Network, Clustering & Enrichment

Expand by adding first-order interactors (100–500 additional nodes), run clustering algorithms to identify functional modules, and overlay biological data to annotate the network.

Clustering algorithms:

MCODE
Most widely used in disease PPI studies. Works by vertex weighting + recursive expansion. Cutoff: score >3, nodes >5. Cytoscape MCODE app.
MCL
Markov Cluster Algorithm. Inflation parameter 1.8–2.5 controls granularity. clusterMaker2 plugin. Good for large networks.
LOUVAIN
Optimises modularity. Best performer in DREAM disease module challenge (Choobdar et al. 2019, Nature Methods). clusterMaker2.

Functional enrichment tools:

STRING built-in: GO (BP/MF/CC), KEGG, Reactome, WikiPathways, DISEASES g:Profiler: GO hierarchy-aware FDR correction — most rigorous Enrichr: 400+ gene-set libraries — broadest coverage clusterProfiler: R package — publication-quality visualisations All: apply Benjamini-Hochberg FDR < 0.05 # Key SCZ enrichment terms to look for: GO:0014069 postsynaptic density GO:0007268 synaptic transmission KEGG: Glutamatergic synapse KEGG: Dopaminergic synapse KEGG: Long-term potentiation

Finally, overlay DLPFC data as node attributes: import DLPFC fold-changes to colour nodes by expression direction, map antipsychotic drug targets, use SynGO synaptic location annotations, and TISSUES for brain specificity scores.

Section 6 · Your Project

Suggested research questions

Eight specific, original, and feasible research questions designed for the Layer 0→1→2 workflow. Click each card to expand the rationale. Feasibility bars indicate how tractable each RQ is for an undergraduate project.

01
GWAS DLPFC

Do GWAS-derived and DLPFC transcriptome-derived SCZ gene sets converge on the same PPI network modules?

PGC3 GWAS hits represent inherited genetic risk, while DLPFC DEGs from CMC/BrainSeq represent downstream molecular changes in disease tissue. Building separate STRING networks from each seed set, then comparing module compositions and enrichment profiles, would reveal whether genetic risk and tissue-level pathology converge on the same protein complexes — or identify novel modules unique to each evidence layer. This directly tests the "common disease module" hypothesis.
Feasibility
HIGH
Read rationale
02
PSD GWAS DLPFC

What are the hub proteins in the intersection of PSD proteins with SCZ GWAS risk genes, and how do DLPFC expression changes map onto them?

The PSD is the primary convergence point for SCZ genetic risk (Fromer et al., 2014; Kirov et al., 2012). Intersecting the Bayés et al. (2011) PSD proteome (~1,461 proteins) with PGC3 prioritised genes (~120), constructing a STRING network, and overlaying DLPFC expression data would identify specific PSD sub-complexes most relevant to SCZ. Highly tractable: all data sources are publicly available. Potential to reveal novel hub proteins beyond DLG4 and GRIN2A.
Feasibility
VERY HIGH
Read rationale
03
PSD GABAergic Immune

Which "bridge" proteins connect the glutamatergic, GABAergic, and immune/complement modules in the SCZ PPI network?

The glutamate, GABA, and neuroinflammation hypotheses are often studied in isolation. A combined network using seed proteins from all three systems (NMDA/AMPA receptors + PV/GAD67/SST interneuron markers + C4A/C3/cytokines) would test whether these converge on shared hub proteins. Betweenness centrality analysis could identify bridge/bottleneck proteins — potentially identifying therapeutic targets addressing multiple pathological axes simultaneously.
Feasibility
HIGH
Read rationale
04
Cross-disorder Network Separation

How does the SCZ PPI disease module overlap with bipolar disorder and autism modules, and which proteins are SCZ-specific?

Cross-disorder genetic overlap is well-established (Cross-Disorder PGC, 2013; Lee et al., 2019), but the protein interaction architecture of this overlap is less explored. Using GWAS-derived seed genes for SCZ, bipolar, and ASD, constructing STRING networks, and computing network-based separation (sAB) scores (Menche et al., 2015) would map shared and disorder-specific disease modules. Proteins in the SCZ-specific neighbourhood are particularly interesting as potential biomarkers or drug targets.
Feasibility
MODERATE
Read rationale
05
Drug Repurposing Network Pharmacology

Which SCZ PPI network hub proteins are targets of existing drugs not currently used in psychiatry — and do they cluster in specific modules?

Ganapathiraju et al. (2016) showed that novel SCZ interactors include targets of 130 existing drugs. Using STRING/STITCH to map approved drug targets onto a SCZ PPI network would identify druggable hubs. If repurposable targets cluster in specific modules (e.g., mTOR signalling, neurotrophin pathway), this would generate hypotheses for drug repurposing based on network pharmacology principles — a topic of high clinical relevance.
Feasibility
MODERATE–HIGH
Read rationale
06
Mitochondrial DLPFC Synaptic

Are mitochondrial and oxidative stress proteins a distinct module within the SCZ DLPFC PPI network, and which proteins bridge them to the synaptic module?

Mitochondrial dysfunction is among the most robust proteomic findings in SCZ DLPFC (Koopmans et al., 2026; Aryal et al., 2023), yet its relationship to synaptic pathology at the protein interaction level is poorly characterised. Seeding a STRING network with both synaptic SCZ genes and mitochondrial DEGs from DLPFC proteomics would reveal whether these form a single interconnected module or separate clusters, and identify bridge proteins — likely including DISC1, which localises to mitochondria-associated ER membranes.
Feasibility
HIGH
Read rationale
07
DLPFC Subtypes Precision Psychiatry

Do "Type 1" and "Type 2" SCZ DLPFC subtypes (Bowen et al.) have different hub proteins and functional modules in their PPI networks?

Bowen et al. (2019) found ~55% of SCZ patients have a dramatically different DLPFC transcriptome (3,092 DEGs, complement upregulation), while ~45% look nearly identical to controls (only 4 DEGs). Building separate PPI networks for these subtypes would test whether SCZ's network architecture is heterogeneous — and could identify subtype-specific hubs and druggable modules, directly advancing the precision psychiatry agenda. This is among the most original RQs available.
Feasibility
MODERATE
Read rationale
08
DISC1 Hub Connector DLPFC

Does DISC1 function as a "connector hub" linking otherwise separate SCZ-relevant PPI modules within the DLPFC network?

DISC1 has ~120 known interactors spanning Wnt/GSK3β signalling, cAMP/PDE4B, neuronal migration (NDEL1), and mitochondrial function. Despite not reaching GWAS significance itself, the DISC1 interactome gene set is enriched in GWAS analyses. Using DISC1 as a focal node within a broader DLPFC SCZ network would test whether it functions as a connector hub bridging multiple pathological processes — a network property that would explain its outsized influence on SCZ biology despite modest single-gene effect size.
Feasibility
HIGH
Read rationale
